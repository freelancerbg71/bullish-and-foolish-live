Flying Cars: Are the Fundamentals Grounded?

United Airlines says yes. The 10-Q says... wait, where’s the revenue?

Every few decades, the world gets a collective fever dream about flying cars. It usually starts with a flashy demo video and ends with a bunch of investors wondering where their money went.

Archer Aviation (ACHR) is the latest and greatest attempt to make it real. They want to fly you from Manhattan to Newark in ten minutes while you sit in a sleek, electric pod that looks like something out of a 2040 cyberpunk flick.

"I’ve always said my dyslexia makes me cautious. When I see a company that has $6 billion in 'indicative' orders but $0 in actual revenue, my internal journalist starts asking questions that finance guys usually ignore."

Because here is the cold, hard, math-induced reality of Archer: they are burning cash like it’s a competitive sport. Managing a startup is hard. Managing a startup that has to build flying hardware and get the FAA to agree it won't fall out of the sky is a whole other level of insanity.

The Big Backers

If Archer were just an idea, it wouldn't be worth a post. But they’ve got United Airlines and Stellantis in their corner. They’ve got actual aircraft taking flight. This isn't just a sketch on a napkin; it’s a high-stakes bet that the FAA will clear them for takeoff in 2025.

But my background as a journalist tells me that partnerships are often just social proof for companies that need more capital. United Airlines "ordering" aircraft is great, but until Archer delivers and gets paid, that money is imaginary.

The Apperceptive Mass Challenge

In my process, I look for stocks that have a big "cushion." Archer has no cushion. If the FAA delays their certification by six months, the balance sheet could snap. It’s a binary outcome: they either revolutionize transport or they become a footnote in a future post about why I don't buy pre-revenue tech.
